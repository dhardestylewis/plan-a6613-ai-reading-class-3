**Author**: Rob Walker (City Tech column, *Land Lines* magazine)
**Publisher**: Lincoln Institute of Land Policy
**Date**: 2024
**Type**: Policy Think-Tank / Academic
**URL**: https://www.lincolninst.edu/publications/articles/city-tech

## Key Content (Verified via Web Search)

Rob Walker's "City Tech" column for the Lincoln Institute's *Land Lines* magazine, and his 2024 book *City Tech: 20 Apps, Ideas, and Innovators Changing the Urban Landscape*, cover how AI is reshaping urban planning processes.

### Core Claims

1. Cities are experimenting with AI to address "tedious parts of city planning" such as construction permitting, zoning code compliance, and development application reviews.

2. Honolulu implemented an AI bot to help with a permit prescreening checklist, significantly reducing wait times for applicants.

3. AI tools are helping bridge the gap between dense, static legal documents and actionable data through Natural Language Processing (NLP) and machine learning, converting municipal codes and zoning ordinances into machine-readable formats.

4. City officials emphasize that AI tools are "not intended to replace human professionals." The goal is to use AI to support, rather than automate, complex decision-making processes.

5. There is a "hype cycle" around AI. Potential risks include the need for data standards, concerns about equity, and the inherent difficulty of building systems that accurately navigate complex, detail-intensive local zoning and building codes.

6. The technology is not yet a "transformative" force for the entire planning system and requires cautious, human-led implementation.

7. The 2025 and 2026 Lincoln Institute / APA trend reports identify AI as a dominant theme, focusing on the need for planners to "upskill" and the necessity of balancing digital AI-driven engagement with face-to-face community interaction.

8. Many AI applications are still in pilot phases. Practitioners are advised to balance hype with reality.
