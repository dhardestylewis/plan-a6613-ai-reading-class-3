# AI Demo Script: TestFit, Real Estate Feasibility, and the Changing Politics of Site Planning
**Course:** PLAN A6613: AI and the Future of Cities  
**Presenter:** Daniel Hardesty Lewis  
**Date:** April 7, 2026 (AI and City Building)  
**Target Time:** 5 Minutes 

---

## ⏱️ Slide 1 | Title (0:00–0:30)
**Visual:** Full-bleed wireframe city with financial dashboards, dark overlay with title.

> "Today I want to look at a tool called TestFit and use it to ask a question about how early-stage site planning decisions are shifting."

---

## ⏱️ Slide 2 | What TestFit Claims to Do (0:30–1:30)
**Visual:** TestFit 3D massing screenshot + vendor quote.

> "TestFit is a real estate feasibility platform. It generates building layouts under zoning, parking, and financial constraints, and it does it in seconds rather than weeks. It integrates with tools like Revit for downstream design.
>
> Notice the language on their own site: 'Win Profitable Deals.' 'Validate your deals with real-time insights into design, cost, and constructability.' The unit of analysis is a *deal*, not a building or a neighborhood. That framing matters."

---

## ⏱️ Slide 3 | Design Through Financial and Regulatory Parameters (1:30–2:30)
**Visual:** Two-column: deal filters interface + algorithmically generated unit layout.

> "Here's how the software actually works. On the left, you see the Deal Solver interface. Users set filters for FAR, building height, parking ratio, and efficiency percentage. On the right, the algorithm generates the corresponding unit layout.
>
> The software privileges financial and regulatory parameters as the primary drivers of early-stage design. Users adjust yield and constraints; the algorithm generates the physical form. That's a meaningful shift in how the earliest design decisions get made."

---

## ⏱️ Slide 4 | Included and Excluded Values (2:30–3:45)
**Visual:** Quantity takeoff dashboard + two-column checklist.

> "This slide is the core of my argument. On the left is the quantity takeoff dashboard: acreage, FAR, units, beds, efficiency, height, parking stalls, ratio. These are the values the software can represent and optimize.
>
> On the right, I've listed values that are not primary optimization targets within this framework: contextual design considerations, displacement risk, affordability, community preferences, environmental or public health impacts. Some of these *could* be encoded as constraints. That they are not is a design decision, not a technical limitation.
>
> To be clear: rapid feasibility analysis can reduce design waste, reveal viable housing capacity, and speed iteration. The question is not whether optimization is useful, but which values get encoded and whose objectives the optimization serves."

*Note: The first three slides use vendor documentation and product screenshots. The implications that follow are my interpretation.*

---

## ⏱️ Slide 5 | Who Has Access? (3:45–4:30)
**Visual:** Full-bleed illustration (planner at desk vs. wall of screens), with text overlay.

> "The next question is access. In practice, tools of this kind are more readily adopted by developers and large architecture firms than by municipal planning departments or community organizations.
>
> This can shift bargaining power. A developer can iterate numerous compliant configurations before a public hearing, while the reviewing agency evaluates one submission at a time.
>
> Planners *could* adopt similar tools, and some are starting to. The question is whether they have the budget, staffing, and institutional mandate to act before development patterns are set."

---

## ⏱️ Slide 6 | Closing Question (4:30–5:00)
**Visual:** Clean dark slide, single question in cyan.

> "So I want to leave you with this question:
>
> *When feasibility is evaluated through proprietary, high-speed optimization, which values become easier to represent, and which values become harder to defend?*
>
> Thank you."

### 💡 Delivery Notes
1. **Register:** Analytical, not activist. You are presenting evidence, not a verdict.
2. **Slide 4 is your anchor.** Spend the most time here. The ✓/✗ list is where the argument lives.
3. **Mark the evidence boundary.** Say explicitly: "The first three slides use vendor documentation. The governance implications that follow are my interpretation." This makes you sound rigorous.
4. **Concession is strength.** The expanded concession ("reduce waste, reveal viable housing capacity, speed iteration") is specific enough to show you understand the tool's value, which makes the critique much harder to dismiss.
5. **If pushed in Q&A** on whether this is really a problem: "I'm not arguing the tool is harmful. I'm asking whether asymmetric access to this kind of modeling capacity changes the politics of site planning, and whether governance has caught up to that shift."
6. **If pushed on "not primary optimization targets":** "Some of these values can be approximated—affordability as a constraint, environmental review as a compliance check. But they are not what the product's interface foregrounds or what the 'Deal Solver' is designed to maximize. The question is about relative salience within the tool's design logic."
