## Pattern Recognition

### Pattern 1: Concentration Creates Disproportionate Impact
Global data: 1.5% of electricity seems modest. But U.S. is 45% of global share, and within the U.S., nearly half of capacity is in five clusters. The impact cascades from manageable at global scale to severe at local scale.

### Pattern 2: Fiscal Incentive vs. Cost Externality
$600M in tax revenue for one county vs. $16/month rate increases spread across millions of residential ratepayers. The benefits are concentrated and visible; the costs are diffuse and hard to attribute.

### Pattern 3: Jurisdictional Mismatch as Governance Failure
Cities have zoning (where to build) but not rate-setting (what it costs) or transmission siting (how power arrives). The most consequential decisions are made by state PUCs and federal agencies.

### AI Blind Spots
- AI assembled statistics well but did not surface distributional tensions without directed prompts
- Initial outputs presented data center growth as a neutral or positive trend
- The equity dimension and jurisdictional gap required explicit stakeholder-framed follow-up
- AI did not independently connect the concurrent existence of $600M tax revenue and $16/month ratepayer burden
