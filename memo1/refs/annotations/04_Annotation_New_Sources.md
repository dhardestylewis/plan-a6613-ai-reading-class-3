# Annotation: New Sources for Temporal Lock-In Thesis

## Date: 2026-03-03
## Thesis: AI energy infrastructure creates a temporal lock-in via four interlocking timescales

## 1. Unruh (2000) — Theoretical Foundation

### Why Selected
Provides the canonical academic framework for understanding infrastructure lock-in in energy systems. Our temporal lock-in thesis is a specific application of Unruh's "Techno-Institutional Complex" (TIC) concept.

### Key Passages for Thesis
- **Path-dependent increasing returns**: "Industrial economies have become locked into fossil fuel-based energy systems through a process of technological and institutional co-evolution driven by path-dependent increasing returns to scale."
- **Self-reinforcing**: TIC is self-reinforcing; "internal changes are unlikely, and external forces may be required to escape"
- **Persistent failures**: Creates "persistent market and policy failures" that hinder adoption of alternatives

### How Used in Memo
- Cited in opening thesis statement to academically ground the "lock-in" concept
- The four-timescale ratchet maps to Unruh's TIC: technology (GPU cycles) co-evolves with institutions (contracts, tax structures, data regimes)

## 2. Lincoln Institute (2025) — Fiscal Lock-In Evidence

### Why Selected
Provides the strongest single data point for fiscal lock-in depth: Loudoun County's DC tax revenue approaching its entire operating budget. Stronger than previously cited Cardinal News $600M FY2023 figure.

### Key Data for Thesis
- **$900M FY2025**: "Loudoun County anticipates nearly $900 million in data center tax revenue in FY2025"
- **38% of general fund**: Data centers generate 38% of all general fund revenues
- **Revenue Stabilization Fund**: County created this in 2023 — evidence they KNOW they're dependent
- **Coal mine comparison**: Kim Rueben compared DCs' "extractive nature" to coal mines — communities "may not fully acknowledge all the associated costs"

### How Used in Memo
- Replaced Cardinal News (2024) $600M figure with $900M FY2025 in Local Government section
- Strengthens "first stage of lock-in is fiscal" argument: once revenue = almost your entire budget, exit is impossible

## 3. Pipa & Aley (2026) — Brookings Metro, Rural Data Centers

### Why Selected
Most directly complementary to our temporal lock-in argument among all reviewed policy sources. Identifies the SYMPTOMS (compressed timelines, asymmetric negotiations, short-term vs long-term trade-offs) that our thesis EXPLAINS.

### Critical Correction
Initial citation was to Turner Lee & West (2025) "The future of data centers" — which covers broad national topics (energy, minerals, tariffs) but NOT municipal temporal lock-in. Full read revealed the relevant quotes come from Pipa & Aley (2026) on rural communities.

### Key Passages for Thesis
- **Compressed timelines**: "compressed timelines, and confidential negotiations" force high-pressure decisions
- **Short-term vs long-term**: "How can local leaders weigh short-term fiscal gains against long-term infrastructure and public service obligations?"
- **Asymmetric negotiations**: "negotiations with developers are often asymmetrical"
- **Long-run obligations**: "long-run obligations, even as technological innovation may change needs and uses"
- **Limited authority**: "Local governments generally have limited authority over these impacts"

### How Used in Memo
- Cited in Looking Ahead section: communities face "compressed timelines and asymmetric negotiations" forcing "short-term fiscal gains against long-term infrastructure and public service obligations"
- Brookings identifies symptoms; our thesis explains the four-timescale mechanism
