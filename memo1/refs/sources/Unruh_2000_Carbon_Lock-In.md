# Unruh, G. C. (2000). Understanding carbon lock-in. Energy Policy, 28(12), 817–830.

## Source Type
Academic journal article (Energy Policy)

## Full Text Access
Not freely available online; accessed via academic search summaries and secondary citations.

## Key Arguments

### Central Thesis
Industrial economies have become "locked into fossil fuel-based energy systems through a process of technological and institutional co-evolution driven by path-dependent increasing returns to scale." This creates persistent market and policy failures that hinder adoption of carbon-saving technologies despite their environmental and economic advantages.

### Techno-Institutional Complex (TIC)
Unruh introduces the concept of the "Techno-Institutional Complex" (TIC) to explain how lock-in arises from "combined interactions among technological systems and governing institutions." Large technological systems (electricity generation, distribution, end-use) are not discrete artifacts but complex systems embedded within a "conditioning social context of public and private institutions."

### Path Dependency and Increasing Returns
The lock-in is driven by path-dependent increasing returns to scale:
- Initial infrastructure investments create economies of scale
- Institutional frameworks develop to support the dominant technology
- Learning curves and network effects favor incumbents
- Sunk costs make switching increasingly expensive

### Self-Reinforcing Nature
The TIC is self-reinforcing: internal changes are unlikely, and external forces may be required to escape carbon lock-in. The existing system actively resists displacement even when alternatives are technologically and economically viable.

### Relevance to Our Thesis
Unruh's framework directly grounds our "temporal lock-in" argument: just as fossil fuel infrastructure created self-reinforcing dependencies through co-evolution of technology and institutions, data center infrastructure creates analogous dependencies through:
- GPU investment cycles driving grid capacity commitments (technology)
- GS-5 rate classes and long-term contracts (institutional)
- Tax revenue dependencies shaping municipal fiscal structures (institutional)
- Data opacity preventing evidence-based intervention (institutional barrier)

The four-timescale ratchet in our memo is a specific instance of Unruh's TIC applied to AI energy infrastructure at the municipal governance level.
