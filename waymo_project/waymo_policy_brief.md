# Autonomous Vehicles and Urban Infrastructure: A Policy Brief on Waymo's Deployment
**Prepared by:** June, Daniel, Jiayi
**Domain Focus:** Physical Planning

## Executive Summary
As autonomous vehicle (AV) services like Waymo expand their operational domains across major U.S. cities, they present unprecedented opportunities and challenges for urban physical planning. While Waymo has demonstrated significant safety improvements and the potential to bridge transit gaps, its deployment raises critical concerns regarding infrastructure equity and energy consumption. This policy brief outlines key physical planning impacts and provides actionable recommendations for municipal governments to integrate AVs into existing urban frameworks equitably and sustainably.

## 1. Analysis of Physical Planning Impacts

### Equity & Infrastructure Inequity
A 2025 study from UC Berkeley highlights the risk of a "two-tiered" mobility system. Waymo's service zones and infrastructure investments often align with areas that already possess high-quality infrastructure, potentially exacerbating historical economic and racial disparities. Neighborhoods with poorer road conditions or complex, irregular street layouts may be systematically excluded from AV service zones.

### Road Safety & Vision Zero Integration
Waymo's 2026 Safety Impact Report indicates a 92% reduction in pedestrian injuries and a 96% reduction in intersection crashes compared to human baselines. This aligns strongly with municipal Vision Zero goals. However, the physical infrastructure—such as clear lane markings, smart traffic signals, and dedicated drop-off zones—remains a prerequisite for maximizing these safety benefits.

### Energy & Emissions Paradox
MIT's 2025 analysis identifies the "data centers on wheels" paradox. While Waymo's fleet is all-electric, eliminating tailpipe emissions, the massive computational power required for full autonomy is highly energy-intensive. If scaled globally, the energy demand could rival that of all existing data centers, necessitating robust grid planning and renewable energy sourcing.

### Transit Integration vs. Competition
A pilot program with Valley Metro demonstrated that AVs can effectively fill "transit mobility gaps" for seniors and paratransit riders. However, without careful spatial planning, AVs risk cannibalizing existing public transit ridership rather than complementing it, leading to increased VMT (Vehicle Miles Traveled) and congestion.

## 2. Policy & Design Recommendations

To address these challenges, we recommend the following actionable interventions for urban planners and policymakers:

### Recommendation 1: Mandate Equitable Service Zone Expansion
**Policy Action:** Implement a "Mobility Equity Framework" that requires AV operators to link expansion in high-income, high-demand areas with proportional service deployment in underserved neighborhoods.
**Implementation:** 
- Municipalities should condition operating permits on phased expansions into transit-challenged areas.
- Provide targeted municipal subsidies or infrastructure upgrades (e.g., improved road markings) in lower-income areas to facilitate safe AV operation.

### Recommendation 2: Establish "AV-Transit Hubs" for First/Last Mile Connectivity
**Design Action:** Redesign physical infrastructure around major public transit stations to include dedicated, geofenced AV pick-up and drop-off zones (AV-Transit Hubs).
**Implementation:**
- Repurpose existing curbside parking near transit hubs for AV staging to prevent congestion.
- Implement geofencing regulations that price AV trips lower when they originate or terminate at a high-capacity transit station, encouraging complementary use rather than transit substitution.

### Recommendation 3: Grid-Aware Charging Infrastructure Planning
**Policy Action:** Require AV fleet operators to integrate their charging infrastructure planning with municipal utility grid forecasts to mitigate the "data center on wheels" energy impact.
**Implementation:**
- Mandate that AV depots utilize off-peak charging schedules or incorporate local renewable energy generation (e.g., solar canopies) and battery storage at their fleet hubs.
- Establish zoning ordinances that locate large-scale AV depots in industrial zones with sufficient electrical capacity, away from residential grids prone to peak-load stress.

### Recommendation 4: Standardize Digital-Physical Infrastructure (V2I)
**Design Action:** Invest in Vehicle-to-Infrastructure (V2I) communication standards, specifically upgrading traffic signals and intersection layouts to communicate directly with AVs.
**Implementation:**
- Allocate federal and state infrastructure grants to upgrade high-collision intersections with V2I transponders.
- Ensure that data generated by AV-infrastructure interactions is shared with the city (respecting privacy boundaries) to continuously monitor and improve intersection safety and traffic flow.

## 3. Implementation and Monitoring
- **Stakeholders:** Municipal Departments of Transportation (DOTs), AV Operators (Waymo), Public Transit Agencies (e.g., Valley Metro, SFMTA), Utility Companies, and Community Advisory Boards.
- **Monitoring:** Cities should establish a "Mobility Data Dashboard" requiring operators to report anonymized data on service equity (trips per neighborhood), deadheading (empty miles traveled), and transit hub utilization to evaluate the effectiveness of these policies.
