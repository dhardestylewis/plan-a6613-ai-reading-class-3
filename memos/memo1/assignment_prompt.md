The Challenge
AI's rapid growth is creating a massive physical and digital infrastructure challenge. From hosting energy-intensive data centers to building fiber networks needed for equitable internet access, cities are at the front lines of this transformation.

For this memo, your first task is to select one major type of infrastructure investment that cities will need in the AI area:

Energy: e.g., grid resilience, microgrids, energy storage.
Connectivity: e.g., fiber broadband, wireless (cellular/mobile or fixed), satellite connectivity.
Compute: e.g., urban data centers or edge computing facilities.

Memo Structure
I. Infrastructure Selection & The "Physical Layer"
For your chosen infrastructure type (Energy, Connectivity, or Compute), briefly describe the physical deployment required:

What is the tangible "hardware" (e.g., fiber lines, GPUs, battery storage)?

Where is it physically located, and why is it necessary for cities' use of technology? 


II. The Stakeholder Matrix: Objectives and Tensions
Analyze how this infrastructure is built and used through each stakeholder's lens, guided by the following questions:

Local Government: What role does local government play, and what are its objectives? e.g., how does a city balance the drive for "AI for Optimization" (efficiency/cost savings) with the mandate for "AI for Equity" (access/non-discrimination)?

Businesses: How do vendors or investors expect to make money from this infrastructure? e..g, how can long-term infrastructure investments be aligned with the rapid, often volatile "Speed vs. Incremental Evolution" of AI technology?

Residents: Beyond being end-users, how do residents interact with this infrastructure? Are they data-creators? What are the specific risks regarding surveillance fears or algorithmic bias that would cause them to withhold support?

III. The "Digital Layer" & Governance
Drawing on class discussions and readings to date, briefly examine the interface between the physical asset and the data it generates and/or relies upon:

Data Ownership: Who owns the "Digital Layer" of this infrastructure? i.e., who controls the data and how it's used? Is it an open model or a proprietary silo?

Regulatory Powers: What institutions make decisions about this infrastructure? Identify the jurisdictional boundaries (e.g., state-level utility regulation vs. local zoning) that limit the city's ability to regulate topics such as privacy or interoperability.

Looking Ahead: How might cities play a greater role in shaping the deployment and use of this infrastructure? e.g., a new city governance mechanism such as a data trust or procurement standard.
