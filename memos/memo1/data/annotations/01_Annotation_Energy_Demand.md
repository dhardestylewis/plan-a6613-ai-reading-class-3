# Annotation: Energy Demand and Grid Impact
**Sources**: IEA Energy and AI (2025), IEA Tracking Page, Pew Research Center (2025)

## Stakeholder Analysis

### International Agency Perspective (IEA)
- Frames data center growth as manageable at global level ("1.5% of electricity") but emphasizes local concentration risk
- Key tension: "AI-focused data centres can draw as much electricity as power-intensive factories such as aluminium smelters, but they are much more geographically concentrated"
- Notes 20% of planned data center projects at risk of delays due to grid strain
- 50% of U.S. data centers under development are in pre-existing large clusters — compounding concentration risk
- Acknowledges data centers are just one driver among several (less than industrial motors, AC, or EVs globally)

### Research Organization Perspective (Pew/Carnegie Mellon)
- Adds the residential cost dimension IEA does not emphasize
- Carnegie Mellon's 8%/25% bill increase estimates provide the equity lens missing from IEA's supply-side framing
- 183 TWh U.S. figure (>4% of national electricity) makes the domestic impact tangible

## What AI Missed (Relevant to Process Reflection)
- AI assembles global statistics but does not interrogate distributional impacts without prompting
- Initial search results presented data center growth as an opportunity narrative
- The equity dimension (who pays for grid upgrades when data centers drive demand) required directed follow-up queries
- AI did not surface the jurisdictional gap (state PUCs vs. city zoning) without explicit stakeholder framing
