# IEA Source: Energy and AI — Executive Summary
**Source URL**: https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/executive-summary
**Source type**: International agency report (IEA Special Report, 2025)
**Downloaded**: March 3, 2026 via read_url_content

## Key Data Points Used in Memo

### Global Data Center Electricity Consumption
- 415 TWh in 2024, approximately 1.5% of global electricity consumption
- Projected to more than double to 945 TWh by 2030 (slightly more than Japan's total electricity consumption)
- Growth rate: ~12% per year since 2017, more than 4x faster than total electricity consumption growth
- Base Case projects 1,200 TWh by 2035

### Geographic Distribution
- United States: 45% of global data center electricity consumption in 2024
- China: 25%
- Europe: 15%
- Nearly half of U.S. data center capacity is in five regional clusters
- 50% of data centers under development in the U.S. are in pre-existing large clusters

### Scale of Individual Facilities
- A typical AI-focused data center consumes as much electricity as 100,000 households
- The largest ones under construction today will consume 20 times as much
- AI-focused data center is 10x more capital-intensive than an aluminium smelter

### Energy Supply Mix
- Renewables meet half of global growth in data center demand to 2035
- Natural gas expands by 175 TWh to meet growing data center demand (notably in U.S.)
- Nuclear contributes about the same amount; first small modular reactors come online around 2030

### Grid Integration Risks
- ~20% of planned data center projects could be at risk of delays due to grid strain
- Grid connection queues are long; building new transmission lines takes 4-8 years in advanced economies
- Wait times for transformers and cables have doubled in past 3 years
- In advanced economies, data centers account for more than 20% of demand growth to 2030

### U.S. Specific
- Data centers account for nearly half of U.S. electricity demand growth to 2030
- By decade's end, U.S. will consume more electricity for data centers than for aluminium, steel, cement, chemicals, and all other energy-intensive goods combined
