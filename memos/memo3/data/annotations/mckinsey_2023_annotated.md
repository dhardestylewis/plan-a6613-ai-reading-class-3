# Annotation: McKinsey Global Institute (2023) — The Economic Potential of Generative AI

| Line / Quote | Annotation & Verification Note | Synthesis Tag |
|---|---|---|
| "up to 30 percent of hours currently worked across the U.S. economy could be automated by 2030" | Verified finding: acceleration from prior 21.5% estimate (without genAI) to 29.5% (with genAI). The "30 percent" figure is the rounded upper bound. Supports the premise that AI-driven time savings are real and near-term, not speculative. | Productivity Premise |
| Knowledge work disproportionately affected — "reverse skill bias" | Unlike prior automation waves (which hit lower-income manual workers hardest), genAI targets high-wage, high-credential knowledge workers. Directly supports choice of hybrid knowledge workers as the population segment. | Population Plausibility |
| $6.1–$7.9 trillion annual economic benefit | Scale of productivity gains confirms that time-freeing is a structural feature of genAI adoption, not an edge case. | Economic Scale |
| Enhancement rather than elimination for professional roles | McKinsey explicitly argues that for STEM, legal, creative, and business professionals, AI will free hours (reduce time per task) rather than eliminate jobs. This is the mechanism behind the "eight hours" premise. | Time Reallocation Mechanism |
| "accelerate the decline of roles such as office support and customer service" | Confirms the bifurcated labor market: knowledge workers gain time; physical and service workers face job erosion. Grounds the equity analysis in Section II. | Equity Divergence |
