# Source: McKinsey Global Institute (2023)
**Title:** The Economic Potential of Generative AI: The Next Productivity Frontier
**Author:** McKinsey Global Institute
**Date:** June 2023
**URL:** https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/mckinsey-digital/our-insights/the-economic-potential-of-generative-ai

## Key Excerpts and Findings (verified from secondary reporting and McKinsey summary)

Generative AI could automate work activities that account for up to 30 percent of hours currently worked across the U.S. economy by 2030 (accelerated from prior estimate of 21.5 percent without generative AI).

Unlike previous automation waves, generative AI disproportionately affects knowledge work — activities involving decision-making, collaboration, and expertise — rather than routine manual tasks. This represents a "reverse skill bias": higher-wage, higher-credential workers face the greatest automation exposure.

McKinsey estimates the total annual economic benefit of generative AI at $6.1 trillion to $7.9 trillion, driven primarily by productivity gains in knowledge worker tasks.

Key knowledge work functions identified as highest automation candidates: data processing, data collection, communicating outputs, performing research and synthesis, and managing routine administrative workflows.

Generative AI is expected to enhance rather than eliminate most professional roles in STEM, legal, creative, and business domains — but will materially reduce time required per unit of output, freeing discretionary capacity.

## Relevance to Memo 3

This is the primary empirical anchor for the "eight hours" premise. The productivity gain is real and concentrated in the hybrid knowledge worker segment. McKinsey's framing of enhancement-not-replacement supports the time-reallocation scenario (workers keep their jobs; they have more hours freed, not zero hours).
